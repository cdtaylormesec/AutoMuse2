However, I recollected afterwards that if he _had_ been       prevented going, the wedding need not be put off, for Mr. Darcy       might have done as well.”        “Mr. Darcy!” repeated Elizabeth, in utter amazement.        “Oh, yes!—he was to come there with Wickham, you know. But       gracious me! I quite forgot! I ought not to have said a word       about it. I promised them so faithfully! What will Wickham say?       It was to be such a secret!”        “If it was to be secret,” said Jane, “say not another word on the       subject. You may depend upon my seeking no further.”        “Oh! certainly,” said Elizabeth, though burning with curiosity;       “we will ask you no questions.”        “Thank you,” said Lydia, “for if you did, I should certainly tell       you all, and then Wickham would be angry.”        On such encouragement to ask, Elizabeth was forced to put it out       of her power, by running away.        But to live in ignorance on such a point was impossible; or at       least it was impossible not to try for information. Mr. Darcy had       been at her sister’s wedding. It was exactly a scene, and exactly       among people, where he had apparently least to do, and least       temptation to go. Conjectures as to the meaning of it, rapid and       wild, hurried into her brain; but she was satisfied with none.
