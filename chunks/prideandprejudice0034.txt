Every day added something to their       knowledge of the officers’ names and connections. Their lodgings       were not long a secret, and at length they began to know the       officers themselves. Mr. Philips visited them all, and this       opened to his nieces a source of felicity unknown before. They       could talk of nothing but officers; and Mr. Bingley’s large       fortune, the mention of which gave animation to their mother, was       worthless in their eyes when opposed to the regimentals of an       ensign.        After listening one morning to their effusions on this subject,       Mr. Bennet coolly observed,        “From all that I can collect by your manner of talking, you must       be two of the silliest girls in the country. I have suspected it       some time, but I am now convinced.”        Catherine was disconcerted, and made no answer; but Lydia, with       perfect indifference, continued to express her admiration of       Captain Carter, and her hope of seeing him in the course of the       day, as he was going the next morning to London.        “I am astonished, my dear,” said Mrs. Bennet, “that you should be       so ready to think your own children silly.
