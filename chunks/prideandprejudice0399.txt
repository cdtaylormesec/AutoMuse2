He would scarcely be ten       pounds a year the loser by the hundred that was to be paid them;       for, what with her board and pocket allowance, and the continual       presents in money which passed to her through her mother’s hands,       Lydia’s expenses had been very little within that sum.        That it would be done with such trifling exertion on his side,       too, was another very welcome surprise; for his wish at present       was to have as little trouble in the business as possible. When       the first transports of rage which had produced his activity in       seeking her were over, he naturally returned to all his former       indolence. His letter was soon dispatched; for, though dilatory       in undertaking business, he was quick in its execution. He begged       to know further particulars of what he was indebted to his       brother, but was too angry with Lydia to send any message to her.        The good news spread quickly through the house, and with       proportionate speed through the neighbourhood. It was borne in       the latter with decent philosophy. To be sure, it would have been       more for the advantage of conversation had Miss Lydia Bennet come       upon the town; or, as the happiest alternative, been secluded       from the world, in some distant farmhouse.
