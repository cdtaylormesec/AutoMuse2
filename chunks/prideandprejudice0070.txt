How shall we punish him for such a speech?”        “Nothing so easy, if you have but the inclination,” said       Elizabeth. “We can all plague and punish one another. Tease       him—laugh at him. Intimate as you are, you must know how it is to       be done.”        “But upon my honour, I do _not_. I do assure you that my intimacy       has not yet taught me _that_. Tease calmness of manner and       presence of mind! No, no; I feel he may defy us there. And as to       laughter, we will not expose ourselves, if you please, by       attempting to laugh without a subject. Mr. Darcy may hug       himself.”        “Mr. Darcy is not to be laughed at!” cried Elizabeth. “That is an       uncommon advantage, and uncommon I hope it will continue, for it       would be a great loss to _me_ to have many such acquaintances. I       dearly love a laugh.”        “Miss Bingley,” said he, “has given me more credit than can be.       The wisest and the best of men—nay, the wisest and best of their       actions—may be rendered ridiculous by a person whose first object       in life is a joke.”        “Certainly,” replied Elizabeth—“there are such people, but I hope       I am not one of _them_.
