I quite detest the man.”     Chapter 4        When Jane and Elizabeth were alone, the former, who had been       cautious in her praise of Mr. Bingley before, expressed to her       sister how very much she admired him.        “He is just what a young man ought to be,” said she, “sensible,       good-humoured, lively; and I never saw such happy manners!—so       much ease, with such perfect good breeding!”        “He is also handsome,” replied Elizabeth, “which a young man       ought likewise to be, if he possibly can. His character is       thereby complete.”        “I was very much flattered by his asking me to dance a second       time. I did not expect such a compliment.”        “Did not you? _I_ did for you. But that is one great difference       between us. Compliments always take _you_ by surprise, and _me_       never. What could be more natural than his asking you again? He       could not help seeing that you were about five times as pretty as       every other woman in the room. No thanks to his gallantry for       that. Well, he certainly is very agreeable, and I give you leave       to like him.
