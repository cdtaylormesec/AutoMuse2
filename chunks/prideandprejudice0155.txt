Miss Bingley, I am sure,       cannot. She is not such a simpleton. Could she have seen half as       much love in Mr. Darcy for herself, she would have ordered her       wedding clothes. But the case is this: We are not rich enough or       grand enough for them; and she is the more anxious to get Miss       Darcy for her brother, from the notion that when there has been       _one_ intermarriage, she may have less trouble in achieving a       second; in which there is certainly some ingenuity, and I dare       say it would succeed, if Miss de Bourgh were out of the way. But,       my dearest Jane, you cannot seriously imagine that because Miss       Bingley tells you her brother greatly admires Miss Darcy, he is       in the smallest degree less sensible of _your_ merit than when he       took leave of you on Tuesday, or that it will be in her power to       persuade him that, instead of being in love with you, he is very       much in love with her friend.”        “If we thought alike of Miss Bingley,” replied Jane, “your       representation of all this might make me quite easy. But I know       the foundation is unjust.
